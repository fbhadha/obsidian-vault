# A Structured Plan for Mentoring

---

## The Core Principle

Mentoring is not advice-giving. It's a relationship built on trust where the mentor asks better questions than the mentee can ask themselves. The best mentors listen more than they talk, challenge more than they comfort, and resist the urge to hand out answers. Sometimes the most useful thing a mentor does is let the mentee struggle with a problem long enough to find their own solution.

The research is clear on one thing: structure matters. Unstructured mentoring relationships drift into pleasant but aimless conversations. Structured ones produce measurable growth. What follows is a plan that moves from the first meeting through ongoing sessions to a deliberate, healthy conclusion.

---

## Before You Even Meet

### What the Mentee Should Prepare

The mentee drives this relationship. That's the single most important norm to establish, and it starts before the first meeting. The mentee should arrive with:

**A brief professional summary.** Not a full CV dump, but a clear picture of where they are right now: their role, their responsibilities, their experience level. If they have a CV or LinkedIn profile, send it to the mentor a few days in advance so the first meeting isn't eaten up by background recitation.

**A rough sense of what they want.** They don't need polished SMART goals yet. But they should be able to answer: *Why did I seek out a mentor? What's the gap between where I am and where I want to be?* If they can't articulate this yet, that itself is a fine topic for the first meeting.

**Two or three questions about the mentor's experience.** This shows respect for the mentor's time and signals that the mentee takes the relationship seriously. Good ones: *What led you to where you are now? What do you wish you'd known earlier in your career?*

### What the Mentor Should Prepare

**Review the mentee's background.** If they've sent a CV or summary, read it. Arrive knowing something about who you're sitting across from.

**Reflect on what you can offer.** Most mentors have more to give than they initially think. Consider not just your expertise but your network, your mistakes, and the lessons that came from them.

**Think about your own boundaries.** How much time can you realistically commit? What topics are you comfortable discussing? What's off-limits? Knowing this in advance prevents resentment later.

## The First Meeting (60–90 Minutes)

The first meeting has one job: build enough trust and clarity that both people want to come back. Don't try to solve anything yet. Resist the urge to dive into content.

### Structure

**Opening: Get to know each other (20–25 minutes).** Share personal and professional backgrounds. Not a monologue from the mentor — this should be a genuine exchange. Find points of connection. The mentor should share why they volunteered their time and what they hope to get from the relationship. (Yes, mentors should get something too. Pretending otherwise makes the relationship feel transactional in the wrong direction.)

**Middle: Set expectations and logistics (20–25 minutes).** This is the housekeeping that most people skip, and then regret skipping. Cover:

- How often will you meet? (Biweekly is the most common cadence; weekly for the first month is even better while you're building rapport.)
- How long will meetings last? (60 minutes is the sweet spot. 90 minutes for the first meeting is fine.)
- Where and how will you meet? (In person, video, phone — pick what's sustainable.)
- How will you communicate between meetings? (Email, Slack, text — and how quickly should the other person expect a response?)
- How long will this mentoring relationship last? Set a rough end date, even if it's provisional. Six to twelve months is the most common range for formal mentoring. A defined time horizon creates urgency and focus. You can always extend.
- Who is responsible for scheduling? (The mentee. Always the mentee.)

**Close: Begin goal-setting (15–20 minutes).** The mentee shares their initial goals and areas where they want guidance. The mentor listens, asks clarifying questions, and begins to understand the mentee's context. Don't finalize goals in this meeting. Instead, agree that the mentee will come to the next meeting with two or three specific goals written down.

**Before you leave:** Schedule the next meeting. Agree on one or two things each person will do before then. The mentee should send a follow-up email within 24 hours summarizing key takeaways and confirming action items.

### The Mentoring Agreement

Many programs use a written agreement, and even informal mentoring relationships benefit from one. It doesn't need to be legalistic. It's a shared document that captures:

- Goals and focus areas
- Meeting frequency, format, and duration
- Communication preferences and boundaries
- Confidentiality expectations (what stays between you)
- How you'll handle it if the relationship isn't working (a "safe exit" clause)
- A proposed end date

Write this up after the first or second meeting. Both people should contribute. It becomes your reference point for the rest of the relationship.

---

## The Second Meeting

This is where the real work begins. The mentee should arrive with:

- Written goals (two or three, specific and time-bound)
- A draft agenda with at least one topic they want to discuss

The mentor's job in this meeting is to pressure-test those goals. Are they specific enough? Are they ambitious enough? Are they actually within the mentee's control? Help the mentee refine vague aspirations into concrete targets. "I want to be a better leader" becomes "I want to lead my first cross-functional project by Q3 and get feedback from at least three stakeholders on my leadership."

Also use this meeting to finalize your mentoring agreement if you haven't already.

## Ongoing Meetings: The Repeating Structure

Every subsequent meeting should follow a rhythm. Not a rigid script, but a pattern that keeps things focused and forward-moving. Here's a structure that works well in 60 minutes:

### Check-in (5–10 minutes)
Brief personal and professional update. How are things going? Any wins to celebrate? This isn't small talk — it builds the relationship and gives the mentor context for what follows.

### Review of action items (5–10 minutes)
What did the mentee commit to doing last time? What actually happened? This is the accountability layer. Without it, mentoring becomes a series of disconnected conversations. If the mentee didn't follow through, explore why — not judgmentally, but curiously. Were the action items unrealistic? Did priorities shift? Is there a pattern of avoidance?

### Core discussion (25–30 minutes)
The mentee brings the topic. It might be a specific challenge they're facing, a skill they want to develop, a decision they need to think through, or a piece of feedback they've received. The mentor's role here is primarily to ask questions, offer perspective, and occasionally share relevant experience.

Good mentor questions during this phase:

- What have you already tried?
- What's the real problem underneath the surface problem?
- What would you do if you weren't afraid of the outcome?
- Who else could help you with this?
- What's the worst-case scenario, and how bad is it really?

### Action planning (10–15 minutes)
Translate the discussion into concrete next steps. What will the mentee do before the next meeting? Be specific: not "I'll work on my communication skills" but "I'll ask my manager for feedback on my last presentation and practise the opening of my next one with a colleague."

### Wrap-up (5 minutes)
Confirm the next meeting date. The mentee should send a follow-up summary within a day.

## What to Discuss Over Time

The topics will evolve naturally, but here are the categories that come up most often in effective mentoring relationships:

**Skills and development.** Identifying gaps, building competencies, finding learning resources, practising new skills. The mentor can help the mentee see blind spots they can't see on their own.

**Career navigation.** Understanding organizational dynamics, evaluating opportunities, making career decisions, building a professional network. Mentors are particularly valuable here because they've already walked the terrain.

**Situational problem-solving.** Working through a specific challenge: a difficult colleague, a stalled project, a high-stakes presentation. The mentor helps the mentee think through options rather than handing them a solution.

**Feedback and self-awareness.** Helping the mentee understand how they're perceived, where their strengths lie, and what patterns might be holding them back. This requires trust and honesty from both sides.

**Confidence and resilience.** Supporting the mentee through setbacks, normalizing struggle, and challenging them to take risks they might otherwise avoid.

## What to Avoid

### The Mentor's Pitfalls

**Talking too much.** The most common mentoring mistake, full stop. If the mentor is doing more than 40% of the talking in any given meeting, something is off. Your job is to draw out the mentee's thinking, not to deliver lectures.

**Giving unsolicited advice.** Ask before you prescribe. "Would it be helpful if I shared how I handled something similar?" is vastly better than launching into a story about yourself.

**Imposing your path.** Your career worked for you. That doesn't mean it's the right template for someone with a different background, personality, and set of circumstances. Guide, don't clone.

**Being too comfortable.** Good mentoring involves productive discomfort. If you're never challenging the mentee, you're being a friend, not a mentor. Push them to set ambitious goals, take risks, and confront the things they're avoiding.

**Solving problems for them.** The whole point is for the mentee to develop their own judgment. If you hand them answers, you're building dependency, not capability.

**Neglecting your own growth.** Mentoring is a skill. Reflect on how you're doing. Seek feedback from your mentee on what's working and what isn't. Read about mentoring. Get better at it.

### The Mentee's Pitfalls

**Coming unprepared.** If you show up without an agenda, a topic, or progress on your action items, you're wasting your mentor's time. The mentor should not have to carry the meeting.

**Expecting the mentor to solve your problems.** A mentor is a thinking partner, not a magic eight ball. Come with your own analysis and ask for perspective, not answers.

**Being closed or defensive.** If you can't be honest about your challenges and mistakes, the mentor can't help you. Vulnerability is what makes mentoring work.

**Not following through.** If you commit to doing something and then don't do it, you're signalling that you don't take the relationship seriously. If circumstances change, say so. Don't just go silent.

**Treating the mentor as a therapist or a friend.** Mentoring has boundaries. Personal challenges are relevant when they affect professional development, but the mentor is not a counsellor. Know when to seek other kinds of support.

### Things Neither Party Should Do

**Let meetings slide.** Consistency matters more than frequency. A missed meeting here and there is normal. A pattern of cancellations kills the relationship. If you commit to biweekly, honour it.

**Avoid difficult conversations.** If something about the relationship isn't working — the cadence, the topics, the dynamic — say so. The mentoring agreement should include a mechanism for this.

**Skip evaluation.** Check in on the relationship itself every two to three months. Are the goals still relevant? Is the format working? Does the mentee feel challenged and supported? Does the mentor feel their time is well spent?

## Frequency and Duration

Research points toward a few guidelines:

**Meet weekly for the first month.** This builds rapport quickly and establishes the rhythm of the relationship. After that, shift to biweekly or monthly depending on the mentee's needs and both people's availability.

**Each meeting should be 60 to 90 minutes.** Shorter than 60 minutes doesn't allow enough depth. Longer than 90 causes fatigue and diminishing returns.

**Plan for at least six months.** Relationships shorter than this rarely build enough trust for meaningful growth. Twelve months is ideal for most professional mentoring. Research on university mentoring found that four or more sessions produced measurably better outcomes than three or fewer — frequency and consistency matter.

**Set a minimum baseline, then be flexible.** Don't force a rigid schedule that creates resentment. But don't let flexibility become an excuse for drift.

## Closing the Relationship

Every mentoring relationship should end intentionally. Ghosting — where one person simply stops showing up or responding — is the worst possible outcome, and it's more common than it should be. An abrupt or unplanned ending can leave the mentee wondering what they did wrong.

### One Month Before the End Date

Review the mentee's original goals and assess progress. Start the conversation about what comes next.

### The Final Meeting

**Reflect together.** What did the mentee learn? What goals were achieved, and which weren't? What would both of you do differently?

**Give feedback.** The mentor should share specific observations about the mentee's growth. The mentee should share what was most and least helpful about the mentoring relationship.

**Discuss what's next.** Will the relationship continue informally? Will you stay in touch professionally? Will the mentee seek a new mentor for their next set of challenges? All of these are legitimate options. Name the one you're choosing.

**Express gratitude.** A handwritten note or a specific, heartfelt thank-you goes a long way. Both parties have invested time and energy. Acknowledge that.

### After the Relationship Ends

The mentee should consider writing a brief reflection on what they learned and what they want to carry forward. The mentor should reflect on what they learned about mentoring and how to improve for next time.

## A Quick Reference for Meeting Cadence

| Phase | Frequency | Duration | Focus |
|-------|-----------|----------|-------|
| First meeting | One-off | 60–90 min | Rapport, logistics, initial goals |
| Second meeting | Within 1–2 weeks | 60 min | Goal-setting, mentoring agreement |
| Month 1 | Weekly | 60 min | Building the relationship, establishing rhythm |
| Months 2–6+ | Biweekly | 60 min | Core mentoring work: skills, challenges, growth |
| Monthly check-in on the relationship | Every 2–3 months | 15 min (within a regular meeting) | Is this working? Are goals still relevant? |
| Final meeting | Scheduled in advance | 60–90 min | Reflection, feedback, closure, next steps |

## What Great Mentoring Looks Like

The mentee leaves each meeting with more clarity than they arrived with. They have specific things to do, think about, or try. Over time, they develop better judgment, stronger skills, and greater confidence in their ability to navigate their career. They don't become a copy of their mentor — they become a more capable version of themselves.

The mentor leaves each meeting feeling that their experience and perspective made a difference. They've been challenged to think freshly about problems they once took for granted. They've sharpened their own skills in listening, questioning, and coaching. They've learned something about a perspective or generation different from their own.

Neither party did it alone. That's the point.
